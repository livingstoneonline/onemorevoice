Introduction

Ivens Craft’s parents, William and Ellen Craft, were two of the most well-known abolitionists and fugitives from slavery in the transatlantic world due to their storied escape. Both born into slave status, William and Ellen resolved that no children of theirs would live an unfree life. The “fact that another man had the power to tear from our cradle the new-born babe and sell it in the shambles [...] haunted us,” they wrote. Little did they estimate just how far from slavery they would have to travel to reach freedom.

Parents

William and Ellen were both born in Georgia in the 1820s. Ellen was the daughter of Maria Smith, an enslaved woman, and Major James Smith, the man that enslaved her and her mother. There are many unknowns that remain regarding Ellen’s mother and her family. Maria Smith did not leave any written record that we know of, and James Smith’s archives are silent on the nature of his relationship with Maria. Nonetheless, the fact remains that Major Smith – actively and everyday – enslaved Maria Smith, robbing her of any legal personhood and therefore any ability to even have the option to consent. So, while we can’t at this moment know the details of the relationship, historian Annette Gordon-Reed writes that what we can and do know is the power dynamic that existed. As she writes about the well-known relationship between Sally Hemmings and her enslaver, Thomas Jefferson, “[t]here could never have been consent between Hemings and Jefferson to a sexual relationship because of the unequal power distribution between them.” An enslaver’s power “was such that one could never be sure of Hemings’,” or in this case Maria Smith’s “true desires.” Maria Smith “did not – because she could not – consent,” (Gordon-Reed 2009, 315).

Anonymous, (Left) “Ellen Craft” and “William Craft” (Illustrations, [1872]), E450 .S85, Library of Congress. No known restrictions on publication. William Ivens Craft's parents, depicted in 1872.

When Ellen was a young girl, she was one of many enslaved people that lived and worked on the Smith plantation. The Smith family enslaved 64 individuals in 1830, by 1840 that number reached nearly 80 [1830, 1840 U.S. census]. William Craft was the son of two enslaved parents (Sterling 1988, 5, 10). William and Ellen came of age as the domestic slave trade boomed in the southern states, spread south and west by enslavers into land made recently available by the U.S. government’s genocidal Indian removal policies. William witnessed his enslaver separate and sell each of his family members – father, mother, brother, and sister – all of whom were purchased by different enslavers to work American’s newly acquired land (Craft 1860, 9-10).

In 1837, Smith’s wife, Elizabeth Cleveland Smith, gifted eleven-year-old Ellen to her daughter Eliza (Ellen’s half-sister by birth) as a wedding present, forcing Ellen to move to Macon. It was there in Macon, while enslaved by the Collins family, that Ellen met William Craft (Woo 2023, 15; Sterling 1988, 8).

Anonymous, “Residence of Robert Collins ESQr M. D. Mulberry Street” (Image, [unknown]), Macon Georgia Then and Now. Unknown copyright. Eliza Smight, Ellen's half sister by birth, married Dr. Collins and moved to this home in Macon. It was here where Ellen met her future husband, William Craft.

Shortly after the couple’s 1846 marriage, they planned their escape (McCaskill 2009, 86) from what William later referred to as “the unrighteous & gallding [sic] yoke, which is now crushing over three millions of my unfortunate country men in to a premature grave” (Craft 1853).

A Daring Escape

William and Ellen later reflected on what had prompted them to make their bid for freedom: “[A]bove all, the fact that another man had the power to tear from our cradle the new-born babe and sell it in the shambles like a brute, and then scourge us if we dared to lift a finger to save it from such a fate, haunted us” (Craft 1860, 1-2). The Crafts resolved that any children of theirs would not be born enslaved.

S.A. Schoff, “Ellen Craft in Disguise” (Image, [1860]), Internet Archive. No known copyright. This sketch of Ellen, created for the book written by William and Ellen in 1860, depicts her as she might have looked during the couple's escape. She wore glasses and a hat, used a sling, and garbed herself in the clothes of a man to hide her gender, race, and associated status as an enslaved person.

Ellen, utilizing the fungible nature of the constructs of gender, race, and class, dressed as a bespectacled well-to-do white man and William postured as her enslaved servant. Leaving Georgia in late December, the couple traveled north by steamer and train through Savannah, Charleston, Wilmington, Richmond, Washington, and Baltimore before arriving in Philadelphia on Christmas Day, 1848 (Craft 1860, 29, 35, 80).

Transatlantic Freedom

Yet the Crafts did not feel their freedom secure even in the nominally free states. Congress passed the Fugitive Slave Law on 18 September 1850, as part of the Compromise of 1850. It required that individuals who had been enslaved be forced back into slavery, even if they had managed to escape to the so-called free states. Alleged fugitives from slavery would be brought before a Commissioner, who would receive $10 if the individual was found to be a fugitive, and $5 if the Commissioner deemed the proof insufficient. The Crafts’ “old masters sent agents to Boston,” and even appealed to President Millard Fillmore to aid them in tracking down William and Ellen (Craft 1860, 88, 92).

William Craft, “President Fillmore Enlisted” (Image, [1860]), Internet Archive. No known copyright. In this passage of their book, William and Ellen describe how their former enslaver enlisted the help of the President of the United States, Millard Fillmore, to try and re-enslave the couple who had escaped north to the "free" states.

Despite widespread opposition and resistance to the Fugitive Slave Law in many northern states, the Crafts resolved to put the Atlantic Ocean between their future family and their former enslavers. They decided it “best, at any sacrifice, to leave the mock-free Republic, and come to a country where we and our dear little ones can be truly free” (Craft 1860, 93).
