Introduction

Despite an absence of correspondence among family members, records show that the Craft family remained a closely knit one even as the Atlantic Ocean stood between them. As William Ivens neared the end of his life, he remained committed to his family, on both sides of the ocean.

Transatlantic Reunions

Ellen Craft Crum had sailed from Liberia to England to witness the marriage of her niece, whom she may have never met before, and to visit her brother. (Green 2018, 143; Clark 2021). Ellen – who stayed in the United States – married Howard University graduate, doctor, and later minister to Liberia, William Demosthenes Crum in 1883. Ellen, too, was a leader in her own right. She was one of the founders of the National Federation of Afro-American Women, a forerunner to the National Association of Colored Women’s Clubs (McCaskill 2015, 87; Sterling 1988, 59). At the first National Conference of Colored Women, held in Boston in 1895, Ellen spoke in support of anti-lynching crusader Ida B. Wells, dubbing her “our ‘Joanna of Arc’” (Sterling 1988, 96).

That same year, William Ivens’ son, William Alfred, married a woman named Elizabeth Christian. Another family reunion occurred in the early 1920s, when William Ivens’ brother, Charles, came to London. In the words of William Ivens’ great-grandson, and William Alfred’s grandson, Chris Clark, “They must have had plenty to talk about after all those years” (Copeland 2021; Clark 2021).

William Ivens continued on as “the head of the family” in England. As Martin and Eileen Copeland describe, “it seems that William Ivens was always part of a big group of family [...] It must have been a very close family unit, in those days–as families were” (Copeland and Copeland 2022). And the family continued to grow. In 1921, William Ivens lived with two of his grandchildren as well as Claude and Nellie Clark on the east side of Vicarage Farm Road in Hounslow, known as the Kiatta Villas. Claude was a fancy leather goods manufacturer who worked out of their home, while Nellie occupied her time with “home duties.” William Ivens, now 65, had most recently worked as a builders clerk for Holland, Hannen & Cubitts in Chiswick. (“1921 England Census”; Copeland 2021).

Anonymous, “Claud Ball Clark and Ellen Louisa Craft with Children” (Photograph, United Kingdom, [1919]), Martin Copeland Private Collection. Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International. William Ivens prioritized keeping in touch with his family across the Atlantic. A letter from his nephew, Charles (son of Brougham), reveals as much. “I have often wondered about you since my return from the army and our most cordial & quite persistant [sic] correspondence have ceased[,] let’s renew it at least for awhile [sic],” suggested Charles at the end of March, 1926. Charles signed the letter familiarly as “Charlie” and wrote from central Harlem, New York City, where he lived just two blocks from the Apollo Theater.

Charles Craft, “Letter from Charlie Craft to William Ivens Craft” (Manuscript, 28 March 1926), Martin Copeland Private Collection. Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International. This 1926 letter from Charles Craft, Brougham Craft’s son, to his uncle, William Ivens Craft, discloses that the two had a relationship despite being an ocean apart. William Ivens not only valued his family in England, but prioritized staying in touch with his family in the United States, as well.

The uncle and nephew had a warm rapport. “I sincerely trust this letter finds you in very good health although [sic] when a body attains the age you have it[‘]s unfair if you expect to prevent an ache of pain now and then,” poked Charles. “Keep smiling and don’t forget to write me soon,” he said (Craft 1926).

At Home in Hounslow

It was in the small house on a quiet street in Hounslow with his family that William Ivens would pass in his sleep in 1926, at the age of 70 (Green 2018, 138; Clark 2021). Without savings to afford a burial, William Ivens was laid to rest in an unmarked grave on Sutton Lane in Hounslow (Copeland 2021). “William Ivens, when he died,” remembered William Ivens’ great-grandson, Martin Copeland, was buried in a “pauper's grave. My mum took me there not long before she died... she knew where it was, but there was nothing there. Sad, because they had no money. They had no money to bury him” (Copeland and Copeland 2022).

Hope McCaffrey, “Chris Clark at Vicarage Farm Road” (Photograph, London, March 18, 2023), Hope McCaffrey Private Collection. Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International. Chris Clark, great-grandson of William Ivens Craft, outside William Ivens’ last place of residence, 31 Vicarage Farm Road in Hounslow, in March of 2023.

That money was scarce at the close of his life represents the cycles of struggle, success, and loss in the life of an educated man of Black-descent in Victorian England. “Even if they did not fulfill their dreams,” notes scholar Barbara McCaskill, “the Crafts,” including William Ivens, “kept trying” (McCaskill 2015, 89). Rather than a linear story of progress, William Ivens’ life is better characterized through its lessons of determination, hope, and the importance of family.

Lead image commentary: This photograph gives us a streetview of the last house that William Ivens Craft lived in with his family, on Vicarage Farm Road in Hounslow. The homes featured a cozy brick facade with red window trim and were called "Kiatta Villas." Although this was only one of many houses that William Ivens lived in during his life, that he lived here with his children and grandchildren made it home.
