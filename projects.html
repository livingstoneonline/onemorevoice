Overview

This page discusses the phases of development through which One More Voice has passed from its initial launch in June 2020 to the present. The page briefly outlines the objectives and outcomes of each phase, then turns introduces significant current and recent One More Voice projects.

One More Voice Development Phases

The startup phase of One More Voice resulted in the Site Launch Edition (2020-21) and focused on establishing a proof-of-concept version of the site. This version:

  • defined site practices, including the use of digital recycling, agile publication, open peer review;
  • created a network of collaboration that centered the recognition of contributions to the site;
  • gathered and presented a selection of representative archival texts (manuscripts and periodicals) plus other relevant materials (visual materials, book-length works, motion pictures); and
  • tested out a variety of minimal computing strategies for building the site so that it might grow with limited resources while remaining accessible to a wide range of audiences.

The current phase of the project, the New Dawn Edition (2021-present), seeks to build on and expand from the foundation established by the initial phase. This phase centers on two key components. First, we have sought to expand the range of primary materials available from the site plus introduce peer-reviewed critical essays that open up the primary materials to further analysis. At present, these materials all appear on the “Archival Texts” page and include a total some sixty primary texts plus critical essays on creators such as Andries Botha, Frederick Douglass, Philip Cohen Labatt, Menelik II, James Read, Jr., Sechele, NoSuthu Soga Jotelo, and Jan Tzatzoe. Second, we have deepened the project’s engagement with relevant primary materials by launching a series of focused projects. The text below introduced these projects.

Current and Recent Projects

Recovering BIPOC Voices from the Victorian Periodical Press (2021-22)

This project consists of two separately-funded initiatives. Both have similar objectives, center on the work of BIPOC (Black, Indigenous, and People of Color) creators from the Victorian periodical press, and share a commitment to supporting the work of underserved academic populations such as graduate students and contingent faculty.

The first initiative, funded by an Arts & Humanities Research Enhancement Program Grant ($15,000) from the University of Nebraska-Lincoln (UNL), seeks to establish a publishing partnership between One More Voice, the non-profit publishing and teaching platform COVE (Collaborative Organization for Virtual Education), and the Special Collections of SOAS Library. The second, funded by a Field Development Grant ($27,500) from the Research Society for Victorian Periodicals (RSVP), also centers on the publishing partnership between One More Voice and COVE, but includes commercial publisher Adam Matthew Digital (AMD) as the final partner.

The initiatives pursue four key objectives. The initiatives seek to 1) draw on the periodical print holdings of the Special Collections of SOAS Library and the databases of Adam Matthew Digital to identify, critically encode, and publish a minimum of 76 periodical pieces by BIPOC creators; 2) publish a series of seven biographical essays and annotated bibliographies on the most significant of these creators; 3) create two co-authored methodological essays on project outcomes and lessons learned; and 4) professionalize the four graduate students from UNL and Purdue University hired through the grants.

To date, the collaboration with SOAS, the more advanced of the two initiatives at present (fall 2021), has already identified and documented an exceptionally wide range of relevant voices in Victorian-era missionary periodicals and so promises to transform current critical knowledge about the presence of such voices.

Corpus of Africa-Centered Literary Works, 1830-1930 (2021)

➥ View the corpus home page on GitHub

This initiative, funded by the University of Nebraska-Lincoln’s Emergency Research Gap Funding Program ($2000) and a Research Assistantship from the English Department, centered on a computational text analysis project led by graduate student Caitlin Matheis. Over the spring and summer 2020 semesters, Matheis developed a corpus consisting of 62 plain text (TXT) files containing nineteenth- and early twentieth-century literary works in English by 31 different African and British writers.

All together, the One More Voice corpus contains over 6.5 million words of text and encompasses works that cover a variety of regions and locations from across the African continent. The corpus also contains a balance of fiction and non-fiction works, as well as a balance between African- and British-authored works. Additionally, Matheis has included a critical essay that focuses on the application of computational text analysis to the corpus using Voyant Tools and a series of annotated screenshots that explore the corpus using a variety of visualization techniques.

Note: At present, graduate student Raymond Malik (Purdue University) is encoding the corpus for COVE via a 0.5 FTE Research Assistantship. Malik’s work will ultimately lay the basis for an enhanced version of Matheis’ corpus.
