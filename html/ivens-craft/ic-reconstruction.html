Introduction

Never quiescent, the Craft family continued to fight for the lives and livelihoods of Black Southerners. The Crafts aimed to develop a farming school that encouraged cooperation among newly freedpeople and also disrupted the white-controlled labor systems sweeping the former slave states. The Crafts’ efforts, however, met with white supremacist violent backlash.

Dreams of Cooperation

Amidst the family’s transition from England to homesteading back in the United States, Ellen gave birth to a son they named Alfred George, who may have been born before the family left or once they returned to the United States – or, there is a small chance Ellen gave birth at sea. After a brief stay with some friends in Boston, the family traveled south in April of 1870 to Savannah (Sterling 1988, 49, 51; Anonymous 1878g). The Civil War had brought the end of slavery, but not the end of the struggle for freedom, life, and rights for Black Americans.

The confusion and speculation over the birth of Alfred Craft stems from the fact that William and Ellen, uncharacteristically, did not register the birth of their son. As a result, the exact date of Alfred’s birth is another question the descendants still actively research. Martin Copeland, great-grandson of William Ivens, mentions that he has two sources that give 1869 as Alfred’s birth year. Chris Clark, another great-grandson of William Ivens, says, it could have been in England, at sea, or the U.S., but, “[o]ur clique knows it was in the U[S],” they just “don’t yet have the documents” (Copeland 2023b; Clark 2023b). The historian Dorothy Sterling asserts that Alfred was born in England, before the Crafts’ return to the United States. (1988, 49). It is the absence of these seemingly mundane records – such as a missing birth registry – that make the Craft history and genealogy a living and changing story. Alfred later attended the oldest HBCU in the Southern United States, Atlanta University, now Clark Atlanta University (Atlanta University 1886-1888; 1890-1891).

In Georgia, the Crafts helped fund and manage Hickory Hill, a plantation leased by William Thompson “on the Charleston road” in South Carolina, just across the border from Savannah (Anonymous 1878b). Their dream was a farming school that encouraged cooperation among newly freed people and disrupted the coercive, and white-controlled, contract labor system. During the farm’s first year, “a very good crop was raised,” William later reported, and Ellen “taught five or six children and seven or eight adults. There were three families on the place.”

The Burning of Hickory Hill and Founding of Woodville

After only one year of farming, however, Hickory Hill – a farm co-operated and leased by two Black men in Reconstruction-era Georgia – erupted into flames one night. William Craft later reported that “the kuklux [sic] burned the buildings at Hickory Hill.” Ellen remembered the terrifying night. She hurried the children out of the burning building, wearing only their night clothes. She managed to save some of their money from the conflagration (Anonymous 1878b; Anonymous 1878c).

With hardened resolve, William and Ellen next established Woodville Co-Operative Farm School in their home state of Georgia (Sterling 1988, 52). The Crafts also started a free school on the property, which was at the time the only school in Bryan County – a seaboard county consisting of large rice and cotton plantations, and one that had a majority-Black population (Blackett 1983, 61; Blackett 1986, 125).

By 1875, although it had put the family into debt, Woodville seemed to be blossoming. The Crafts built a large schoolhouse attended by upwards of 75 students. At least sixteen families lived on the property, farming cotton, rice, and corn (Craft 1875). Ellen and Brougham taught in the school, seldom receiving compensation. Brougham had earned certificates for teaching from school committees in Georgia and Florida. Though some of the scholars had promised to pay, recalled Brougham, he “never got anything from them, except four red birds” (Anonymous 1878d).

William Craft, “’Primary Education in Georgia’” The Christian Union 8, no. 5 (30 July 1873): 92, Internet Archive. No known restrictions on publication. William Craft penned this article to not only make known his hopes for the Woodville school, but also likely to drum up support, financial and otherwise, for the endeavour.

The Craft family also became involved in Reconstruction politics, joining the Republican Party in Georgia. William Craft, in 1873, applied for the office of Minister to Liberia, “with good prospects” the Augusta Chronicle reported (Anonymous 1873). The next year, he ran for state Senate in Georgia as a Republican (Anonymous 1874c). Both campaigns proved unsuccessful. However, in 1876, William represented Bryan County at the Georgia Republican State Convention. There, he was elected as an alternate delegate to the Republican National Convention (Blackett 1986, 129; Anonymous 1876b). Politics was a family affair. In Savannah, William Ivens’ older brother, Charles, was president of the Union Coterie club (Craft 1876).
