Introduction

Little is currently known or remembered about Louisa Le Jeune Craft – William Ivens’ wife and the mother of his children. What we do know is that when she entered William Ivens’ tumultuous life, so too did a period of calm and stability which, ironically, left few paper traces through which we can understand this time in William Ivens’ life.

Looking for Louisa

Craft descendants recall that family lore had them believe William Ivens rescued a destitute Irish woman and her children from the streets of London. Historical records show us that it was in fact the other way around (Copeland and Copeland 2021). What the family story reflects, however, is how little is known or remembered about William Ivens’ wife. From extant records we see that Louisa, the daughter of John Gilbert Woodley, a gardener-turned-tailor, was born in Hackney around 1853 (“1861 England Census”; “Pallot’s Marriage Index” 1837). Likely named after her Tottenham-born mother, Louisa Spooner, Louisa and her family lived on Holly Street in Hackney throughout the 1850s. Located in north-east London, Hackney was far removed from the Craft home in Hammersmith lived in the 1850s. Though both came of age in London in the 1850s, it was unlikely William Ivens and Louisa crossed paths prior to 1882.

Louisa was sixteen years old at the time of her marriage to Felix Le Jeune in 1869. The young girl scribbled a wobbly “X” on the marriage certificate under Felix Le Jeune’s flourishing signature (“England Marriages and Banns” 1869). The Le Jeune family was able to afford servants in the 1870s, as indicated by a publicized incident. In 1878, Felix and Louisa took a servant of theirs, Annie Church, to court for stealing a gold ring worth six pounds. Louisa testified in court, claiming that she had spotted the ring at a nearby pawnbroker’s shop. The story circulated in The Standard (Anonymous 1878i).

Likely unable to read or write, Louisa may have welcomed the company of someone her own age when William Ivens started boarding at their home in 1882, her husband nearly twenty years her elder. William Ivens’ descendants assume that Louisa was white, as there are no records that have been found yet that indicate otherwise (Copeland and Copeland 2021).

William Ivens and Louisa married in the spring of 1888 in Holborn (“Marriage Index” 1888). Three years later, their family had grown by three more: Clara Eugenie was born in 1888, William Alfred in 1889, and young Brougham – named for his uncle – in 1891. William Ivens found employment doing domestic work. Louisa’s oldest son, fifteen-year-old Charles Le Jeune, worked as a shoemaker’s assistant, and her thirteen-year-old son, Henry Le Jeune, was employed as an “office boy.” Including the children from Louisa’s first marriage, the household numbered ten, and the family moved first to Chelsea and then to 57 New Broad Street, in Bishopsgate (“Register of Birth” 1889; “1891 England Census”).

With three wage-earners, the Craft family experienced a period of relative stability in London – at least in comparison to William Ivens’ tumultuous years of scarcity. Less than three years passed between William Ivens’ return to London and the birth of his first child, but the hardship and poverty he faced in that time must have made the next years of financial stability even more valuable. The more stable William Ivens’ life became, however, the less paper trail he seems to have left behind. As a result, historians must rely more on family and oral histories to trace the years after William Ivens met and married Louisa.
